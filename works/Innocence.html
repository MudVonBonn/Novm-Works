Innocence Moratorium

"Your first love is so sweet, the rest is sugar free. I try so hard to be the girl I was at seventeen. [...] I’ll miss your eyes, your ears and the pimples on your face. I’ll miss the lies you told me, the truth hurts anyway. I’ll miss my high school and every time I got called gay. I’ll miss my part times and every awkward phase.”

I’m going to kill myself.
She said that to me as if her statement was just a wholesome comment about the humid weather or a prosaic complaint about her newfangled responsibilities in the last term of school.
It was a definite statement. Un-euphemistic.
I’m going to kill myself after I finish school, she repeated.
Yeah, I heard you the first time, I said in return.
In a world of miscommunication, isn't a reaction the only true way to know our words, our convoluted arrangements of sound waves, have reached our intended audience?
What, do you want me to cry? I asked.
When I’m gone I expect you to weep.
Our steps were steady, clock-like, as they made us advance through the coastal sidewalk. I was wearing the old sandals my mom had bequeathed me. She was barefoot.
Why are you telling me that? I asked.
I want you to be ready for it. I’m going to do it, I really mean it.
I believe you.
I was pretty tired from walking, but she seemed perfectly content forever moving forwards.
There’s something very cool I have planned to do before I’m gone. I felt a bead of sweat fall down the left side of my face. I want you to join me, she said authoritatively.
In dying?

We walked in silence for approximately an hour. Soon after she said she had to leave.
After we said goodbye she opened her arms to give me a hug. I didn’t really like hugs, but it felt appropriate to hold her dearly against my chest.
Subsequently, I returned home. I walked at a considerably slower pace when she wasn’t by my side to hasten me. I had time to look around my prefecture.
The brick walls permeated with chromatic graffities. The parks filled with truant kids smoking weed. The stray cats loitering in abandoned houses. The names of lovers carved out on wet cement. The bustling streets. The desolate alleyways.
It was all the same as it had always been.
It would continue to be like this even after she was gone.
I didn’t like that thought.
