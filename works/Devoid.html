So Devoid Of Meaning

It’s hard to describe the noise that inundates your brain when you find out your father is dead. A mix of old TV static, the revving of cheap chainsaws and the sound of vomiting in slow motion – it all felt so horribly electric. I cried, of course, when my aunt called me to tell me of his passing. I ran to my bed, repeatedly punched my anime body pillow and tried my best to remember all of the worst moments, all the meaningless screams and piercing insults he threw at me, to stop the cascading pressure of sadness. But my tears did not stop
When I came to, a few hours later, once the twilight mocked the sunset, I found my room made a quiet horrorshow; all my useful post-it notes made a mess. Most of my vinyls were broken in perfect halves strewn about on the floor and every single book I owned had the ten pages in the middle removed and stuck with saliva on my walls. All my bras were on the porch, for some reason..
I rationalized it through the simple and universal thought that grief makes you do some weird shit. Adding to my hypothesis, on the day of my father’s funeral I spent over three hours simply staring at a statue of a baby cupid above the fireplace in the old apartment he owned as a college student. He used to say these ragged walls saw the best life he ever had to offer; before he moved into the big dumb family house and became a big dumb dad. I would also say similar things if I had enough money in college to buy an apartment with a fireplace.
I gave his eulogy sensibly (bla bla bla) and with the appropriate love (bla bla bla), yet, as the kind words left my mouth I could only think of distracting stuff like upside down pink trees and medicinal vibrators and government approved stickers of your first crush.
During the small dinner that was hosted after the main ceremony I thought of the moment he had said he had failed as a father when I unplugged his vape to charge my phone. The food at the funeral tasted fine.
“Would you like to stay here, sweetie?” my aunt asked with a gentle tone.
“I don’t think I loved my dad,” I said, but she did not react. “I don’t think I hated him either. But choosing one of those options feels easier than reaching a compromise.”
“So, you don't want to stay?”
“I…” I closed my eyes, scrunching up my ugly face, pressing my eyelids with all the strength I could summon and shook my head lightly. “I want to stay here tonight. Being here makes me feel very miserable and I like feeling that way, at least for now.”
“It’s okay.”
My aunt smiled and dutifully tidied up all the casseroles and cutlery and tear-filled tissues and the flowered stand with his face beautifully printed in colors that reminded me of memories I did not have. I thought of helping her, but my muscles did not move.
“Bad girl,” I said to myself.
“What the heck?” asked my aunt, seemingly very confused.

It took a few hours for midnight to arrive and a few more for me to finally stand up from the beaten up grey couch and head to the bathroom. I washed my face and put on a set of childish orange pajamas. I looked at the water that had somewhat pooled on the sink and wondered if I could drown there, but the liquid was being drained into the pipes too quickly.
I went into the bedroom and fell asleep watching a documentary about rotating black holes and non-rotating black holes and other things.

When I woke up there was a red button in my father’s room. Weird!
A raven black pillar had appeared in the exact middle of the room, equally separated from all walls. On the top, approximately at the height of my heart was a shiny rose colored button. The material of the pillar was sturdy, impossible to move or break, but I was unable to identify it. The black structure seemingly sprouted from the tiled floor and deep within my entrails I had the inexorable sense that it was alive; and a gurgling fear at the unknowledge of its intentions.
